Studying Child Development

Developmental milestones, from newborn gurgles to first words and beyond, are fodder for research at the new Laboratory for Child Development at Johns Hopkins.



"We investigate how infants and young children perceive and reason about the world around them," explained Lisa Feigenson, PhD, co-director with Justin Halberda, PhD, of the lab in the Zanvyl Krieger School of Arts and Sciences at Johns Hopkins University. "Through our work, we have discovered that in many ways children know much, much more than people once thought they did; but in other ways children perceive the world quite differently than adults do."

Drs. Feigenson and Halberda, pictured above, are assistant professors of psychological and brain sciences.

Topics studied in their lab include how infants and young children keep track of and reason about moving or hidden objects, how children learn words for new objects and actions, and how they understand numbers prior to any formal mathematical education. The studies, which involve children ages 3 months to 6 years, take place in a brightly painted lab decorated in a friendly zoo theme.

"We felt it was very important that the lab environment be cheerful and colorful and be a place where babies, children and their parents could feel at home and at ease," Dr. Halberda said. "We’re interested in how babies and children process information. In order for them to do that in the most natural way possible, they have to be comfortable."

Drs. Feigenson and Halberda, who are married, came to Johns Hopkins from Harvard University last summer to set up and run the new Laboratory for Child Development. dr. Feigenson specializes in the study of how infants keep track of and remember objects, while Dr. Halberda studies word learning and logical reasoning.

Though their research interests diverge, they have the same goal of understanding how infants and young children perceive and contemplate the world around them. They do this through a variety of studies approved by a Johns Hopkins University Institutional Review Board. The studies take the form of simple games that babies, children and even parents—all volunteers from the community—usually find fun and engaging.

"In our infant studies, for instance, a baby sits in an infant seat and watches a kind of show consisting of objects or video animations," Dr. Feigenson explained. "We record the baby’s behavior and measure how long he or she spends looking at or reaching for an object."

What can the researchers learn from such a simple exercises?

"Babies usually look longer at things they find new or surprising, so we can make inferences about how they perceive and understand what they see by looking for patterns of behavior across a number of infants," Dr. Halberda stated. "We are looking at patterns, not performances of individual children."

These patterns can inform parents, pediatricians and scientists about what goes on in the mind of a young child.

"In infants and small children," Dr. Feigenson reported, "we use behavioral patterns to make inferences about babies’ knowledge—not just whether they have or don’t have some bit of knowledge, such as whether they know that things continue to exist even when they can’t see them, but also how that knowledge is structured. Does one bit of knowledge depend on another; does it require experience in the world in order for it to develop; or is it hard-wired into the brain as a product of evolution? Finally, how can a big blob of brain tissue ever contain the complexities of information babies and small children deal with every day? These are just some of the subjects we are interested in exploring."

Johns Hopkins paid for the construction of the new lab facility, and Drs. Feigenson and Halberda are in the process of applying to the National Institutes of Health and the National Science Foundation for funding of their research studies.
