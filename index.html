Welcome to the Vision & Cognition Lab!

Among other things, we're interested in the limits of visual attention and visual working memory, the psychophysics of numerical estimation, and the interfaces between vision and language. We maintain strong collaborative ties with the Laboratory for Child Development and the Visual Thinking Lab, as well as the larger Vision Sciences Group at JHU.

PI

Justin Halberda, Professor

Dr. Justin Halberda directs the Vision and Cognition lab, and co-directs the Laboratory for Child Development with Dr. Lisa Figenson. He currently holds appointments in the Department of Psychological and Brain Sciences (primary) as well as the Department of Cognitive Science. His recent work in this lab is focused on the organization of visual attention and working memory, psychophysical modeling of the human approximate number system, and the semantics of logical vocabulary (e.g., quantifiers).

Hrag Pailian

Hrag defended his dissertation, "Costs of Manipulating Information in Visual Working Memory" in July 2015. In it, he focused on the following questions: is the limit for manipulating information independent of that for storing information and are original representations updated or do they remain separate once they have been subjected to a given computation? To this end, he has developed a novel method - the Flicker paradigm - which allows for storage constraints to be studied within the context of how VWM is flexibly used in the real world. He is currently a Postdoctoral fellow under George Alvarez in Harvard's Vision Sciences Laboratory. Check out his Personal Website

Interested in becoming part of VCL?

Please select a role below.

We welcome applications from high school students.

Typically, this means that your school supports research experiences and you are working towards a final project (e.g., paper or presentation) for your school.

Along with Dr. Halberda, you would be paired with a graduate student or postdoc, who will serve as your mentors.

Typically, you will be working on one particular project and you will learn all aspects related to this project (literature review, designing experiments, programming studies, running participants, analyzing data, making posters or talks that communicate the findings, presenting in the lab or at a conference).

Expectations during the school year are a minimum of 9 hours per week, but can be more.

During the summers, students tend to work closer to 20 hours per week.

By in large, these positions are unpaid. We do not have funds specifically for high school students.

If you will be coming from elsewhere, you must have family to stay with in the Baltimore area.

Please tell us about yourself!
